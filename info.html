Background

On a chilly winter night, a fire in the fireplace is both welcoming and reassuring. At a high temperature, the organic compounds that make up wood react with oxygen to produce carbon dioxide, water vapor, and a lot of heat. An exothermic chemical reaction is one in which heat is released during the process. The heat energy from the fire travels to you as you sit in front of it. Here, you can learn about heat flow and how some chemical reactions naturally release energy while some other reactions absorb energy. You will learn how to show heat changes in chemical equations and perform calculations in order to determine the heat being absorbed or released in all sorts of chemical reactions, changes of state, and other physical processes.

Key Terms

  • Thermochemistry is the study of energy changes that occur during chemical reactions and during changes of state.
  • Heat is energy that is transferred from one object or substance to another because of a difference in temperature between them.
  • Chemical potential energy is the energy stored in the chemical bonds of a substance.
  • Law of conservation of energy states that in any physical or chemical process, energy is neither created nor destroyed.
  • System is the specific portion of matter in a given space that is being studied during an experiment or an observation.
  • Surroundings is everything in the universe that is not part of the system.
  • Heat capacity is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of an object by 1°C.
  • Calorimetry is the measurement of the transfer of heat into or out of a system during a chemical reaction or physical process.
  • Enthalpy (H) is the heat content of a system at constant pressure. It is impossible to measure the enthalpy of a system, but we can look at changes in enthalpy.
  • Heat of reaction is the enthalpy change for a chemical reaction.
  • Thermochemical equation is a chemical equation that includes the enthalpy change of the reaction.
  • Hess’s law of heat summation states that if two or more thermochemical equations are added together to give a final equation, then the heats of reaction for those equations can also be added together to give a heat of reaction for the final equation.
  • Heat of combustion is the heat released when one mole of a substance is completely reacted with oxygen gas.
  • Standard heat of formation (ΔHf°) is the enthalpy change associated with the formation of one mole of a compound from its elements in their standard states.

Similarly, Reaction B is exothermic because the products have less heat energy than the reactants. This is also seen in the change in enthalpy. Exothermic reactions result in a negative enthalpy, while endothermic reactions result in a positive enthalpy.

Key Concepts

  • Chemical reactions are accompanied by transfers of energy. It is important to keep track of heat flow and energy requirements for a full understanding of chemical processes.
  • Heat flow is measured in one of two common units: the calorie and the joule.
    • The calorie was originally defined as the amount of heat required at a pressure of 1 standard atmosphere to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water 1° Celsius. Since 1925 this calorie has been defined in terms of the joule, the definition since 1948 being that one calorie is equal to approximately 4.184 joules.
  • The standard formula for determining heat flow is: q = m × c × ΔT
  • The energy changes of a system occur as either heat or work, or some combination of both.
  • Heat always flows from an object at a higher temperature to an object at a lower temperature. The flow of heat will continue until the two objects are at the same temperature.
