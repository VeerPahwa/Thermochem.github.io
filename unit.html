Stoichiometry and Thermochemistry

Stoichiometry is the relationship between the relative quantities of substances taking part in a reaction or forming a compound.

Stoich allows us to convert from moles of one substance to moles of another. There is a direct proportionality between these substances given the chemical equation. Using this same proportionality, we can calculate how much heat is needed given a certain amount of moles of a substance.

Thermochemistry also connects to the more basic foundations of chemistry, such as dimensional analysis and balancing equations

For example, given the equation: ___H2O (g) + ___CO2 (g) + 212.8 kcal --> ___CH4 (g) + ___O2 (g), find how much heat is needed to produce 13 moles of methane.


First, balance the equation. The result is 2 H2O + 1 CO2 + 212.8 kcal --> 1 CH4 + 2 O2

Just like stoich, we can treat the 212.8 kcal as essentially another molecule. For every 212.8 kcal inputted into the reactants, there is 1 mole of methane produced.
Using this relationship, we arrive at our answer of 13 mols * 212.8 kcal/mol = 2766.4 kcal



Thinking of heat as a part of the chemical equation also has connections to equilibrium. For example, in the previous question, heat was placed on the side of the reactants. This would indicate that the reaction is endothermic, as the products absorb this heat (positive change in enthalpy).

Thus, for example, assuming the forward and reverse reactions have reached equilibrium, we can use this knowledge to know that if heat is added to this system, the forward reaction is favored (or the products are favored).
